## This Week's Term: Bandwidth

**The quick version:** How much data can flow through a connection at once. Think of a highway â€” more lanes means more cars can pass. More bandwidth means faster downloads.

**The deeper version:**

Imagine a highway. A one-lane road can only handle a few cars at a time. A six-lane highway can handle a lot more. Bandwidth is the number of lanes on your internet highway â€” how much data can flow through at once.

High bandwidth means you can download a movie in seconds, stream 4K video without buffering, and video call without freezing. Low bandwidth means everything crawls. If you've ever been in a house where everyone's streaming Netflix at the same time and the internet slows to a stop â€” that's a bandwidth problem. There's only so much highway to go around.

People often confuse bandwidth with speed, but they're slightly different. Bandwidth is capacity (how much), latency is delay (how fast). Think of it like a water pipe: bandwidth is the diameter of the pipe (how much water can flow), latency is how long it takes a drop to get from one end to the other. You want both â€” big pipe AND fast flow.

## The Nerd Corner

Bandwidth is the maximum data transfer rate of a network connection, measured in bits per second (Mbps, Gbps). It's determined by the physical medium (fiber > cable > DSL > satellite), network equipment, and ISP provisioning. Throughput is the actual achieved rate, which is always less than theoretical bandwidth due to protocol overhead, congestion, and packet loss. QoS (Quality of Service) policies prioritize traffic types. Bandwidth-delay product (BDP) determines optimal TCP window sizes for efficient utilization.
